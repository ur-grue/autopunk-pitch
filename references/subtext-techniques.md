# Dialogue Subtext Techniques

## The 5 Core Techniques

### 1. Deflection
Character answers a different question than was asked.
Reveals: avoidance, discomfort, hidden agenda.

Example:
"Did you love him?"
"The coffee here is terrible. Want to try that place on Fifth?"

### 2. Contradiction
Words say one thing, actions/body language say another.
Reveals: internal conflict, dishonesty, self-deception.

Example:
"I'm fine," she said, gripping the edge of the table hard enough to whiten her knuckles.

### 3. Silence
What is NOT said carries meaning.
Reveals: pain too deep for words, power dynamics, guilt.

Example:
"Do you ever think about what happened in Portland?"
He picked up his fork. Set it down. Picked it up again.

### 4. Interruption
One character cuts off another.
Reveals: power dynamics, urgency, fear of what will be said.

Example:
"I was going to tell you about—"
"The quarterly numbers. I know. I've seen them."

### 5. Non-Sequitur
Character changes subject entirely.
Reveals: avoidance, emotional overwhelm, disassociation.

Example:
"The divorce papers came today."
"Did you know there are over 300 species of hummingbird?"

## Dialogue Tag Rules

- Default to "said" — it's invisible to readers
- Use action beats instead of tags when possible:
  BAD: "I can't do this," she said nervously.
  GOOD: She set the glass down too hard. "I can't do this."
- Never use: "exclaimed", "retorted", "queried", "opined", "mused"
- Never modify "said" with emotion adverbs: "said softly", "said angrily"
  Instead, show the emotion through action or context
